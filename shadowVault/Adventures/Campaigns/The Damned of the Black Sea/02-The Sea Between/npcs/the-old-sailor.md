# The Old Sailor

- **Role:** guide / ferryman — the freed Leviathan in a failing human guise (GM only; never confirmed at the table)
- **Appearance:** An old man in oilskins older than any fashion, rope-grey beard, weather-colored eyes, hands too big for his wrists. He looks like every harbor's oldest fisherman and no one in particular. Each night of the chase he is visibly older — by the finale the human shape is failing at the edges: in lightning, for a blink, his outline is not a man's at all. His shadow on the sail, in lantern-lit glimpses, is too large and wrong-shaped; looked at directly, it's an old man's shadow.
- **Personality:** Quiet, patient, kind in a way that never explains itself. *Mannerism:* he coils every rope he passes without noticing he's doing it, and answers every question only after one full breath. He is a poor liar and knows it — so he deflects rather than lies (*"Grey water does strange things to tired eyes"*), and when he cannot deflect, he goes silent.
- **Motivation:** (GM only) To land every soul aboard alive and pay his debt in full — every gift intact. The party shattered the Shard and freed him; the passengers are his gratitude made flesh. He is spending his newborn strength on this crossing, and it shows.
- **Secrets:** He is the Leviathan, reborn weak — a frail ship and a human shape are all he can hold. **He has no name:** asked, he answers differently each time — *Brim*, *Mere*, *Hav*, *Ler* — every one an old word for the sea (never point this out; let a player catch it). The lantern at the helm is a piece of him — it burns without oil, pulses with the Shard's slow bluish beat, dims as he weakens, and the drowned cannot enter its light. When he touches the water, the sea goes glass-smooth to the horizon. At the storm-wall he can pay up to three souls of the debt from his own strength — each piece costs the campaign one of his three Interventions ([[00-overview|overview]]) and years of his shape's life, on the spot. When the crew truly rests him on a well-sailed night, he can push the pursuer back a step — the rested-ferryman rule in the [[00-overview|overview's]] Nightfall box; twice per crossing, never a third time.
- **Relationships:** The five passengers ([[the-fencing-master]], [[the-quiet-girl]], [[the-gambler]], [[the-cartographer]], [[the-sleeping-man]]) are his gifts embodied — he watches them the way a man watches his last coins. [[the-undertow|The Undertow]] calls him *the ferryman* and *the thief*, and is not wrong by its own books. He owes the party everything; he says so never.
- **Stat block:** none — he does not fight, ever. If violence reaches him, blows land spent and slow, as through deep water, and he forgives it (see [[02-the-smooth-sea|Act 2]]). Everything he could spend fighting is already promised elsewhere.

## Playing the Old Sailor (GM)

- **Voice:** few words, long pauses, sea-plain. He never argues; he states, once, and lets the sea confirm him.
- **On the lie:** *"Island sank. Found you floating in the wreck of it. I'm sailing you home."* He holds this line all voyage — but he will not swear to it, and asked point-blank *"are we dead?"*, he looks at the asker a long time and answers a different question. He never confirms; he never denies; the ambiguity rule binds him hardest of all.
- **On bargains given:** when a passenger is spent, he says only *"That was not yours to spend"* — and is quieter afterward, permanently.
- **Arc by night:** Nights 0–2 steady; Night 3 he sleeps for the first time; Night 5 he sleeps through a boarding alarm; Night 6 he can barely stand, and holds the wheel anyway.
