# The Sea Between — Concept

> [!success] Built
> This concept has been built: **[[02-The Sea Between/00-overview|The Sea Between]]** (built 2026-07-12, against a generic party of 4 — fighter, priest, thief, wizard). This file stays as the record of intent.

> Working title. Adventure II of *The Damned of the Black Sea*, following the destroyed-Shard branch of Adventure I. The old Adventure II source hints (anchor-bound ghost pirates, Black Sea cultists, soothsayer of the "Möwe", the First Pirate's crypt) are **dropped entirely** — Adventures II and III are rebuilt fresh from the new situation.

## Logline

Killed by their own captain for destroying the Shard of the Deep, the party wakes aboard an impossible little fishing ketch on a windless grey sea, where an old sailor — the freed Leviathan in human guise — claims he fished them from the wreck and is sailing them home. Behind them, one step closer each night, comes the Undertow: every ship the sea ever swallowed fused into one patient mountain of hulls, politely insisting that drowned souls are owed to it and the ferryman is a thief. To reach the dawn they must keep the failing ship sailing, refuse the fog's sweet bargains, and at the storm-wall decide what — or whom — to feed to death's law, never knowing for certain that they are dead.

## Premise (player-facing)

**Fixed by the seed (prologue):** The party destroyed the Shard of the Deep. The island convulses in earthquakes; the collapsing temple forces them back to the Chamber of Splintered Truth, where the vision pool has become a passage — they dive through and surface at the beach. There, Captain Rauthorn and the remaining crew are fighting off drowned sailors roused by the Leviathan's rebirth. When the party tells Rauthorn what they did, he attacks. The fight **cannot be won** — the party dies, ending the prologue.

They awaken aboard an old ship on a smooth, windless sea, steered by a lone old man. His story: *the island sank, he found them floating in the wreckage, and he is sailing them home.* A plausible lie the players can cling to — which makes every crack in it hit harder. The rest of the adventure is their journey back to life. **They are never told they are dead — they may only ever suspect it.**

**The cracks (signs the players may read, never confirmed):**
- **The wounds that killed them** — each character bears one mark from the beach fight (Rauthorn's saber cut, a drowned sailor's grip) that neither bleeds, heals, nor hurts. It aches only when the pursuer is near.
- **No hunger, no sleep** — they eat from habit but never hunger; they sleep but never dream and never tire. Rations aboard never run out — and never taste of anything.
- **The sky is wrong** — no sun, only fixed grey twilight; unknown constellations; the ship's wake never fades, stretching to the horizon like a scar.
- **Reflections misbehave** — in water, mirrors, and blades their reflections lag a half-second behind, or briefly show them as they looked at the moment of death on the beach.

## The Truth (GM only)

The old sailor is the Leviathan in human guise, freed when the party shattered the Shard. Out of gratitude it ferries their souls across the sea between death and life, guiding them back to the living world as its reward.

**Why a voyage at all:** The Leviathan is a god of the sea, not of death — it can sail the border but not overrule death's law. It is *smuggling* their souls back, and the pursuer in the ship's wake is death's law coming to collect what was taken. Worse: newly reborn from the shattered Shard, the Leviathan is still weak — a frail ship and a human shape are all it can hold. At full power the crossing would take a night; weakened, it takes the whole adventure. (The Leviathan regathering its strength is a campaign thread that carries into later adventures.)

**Chosen direction — "The Ship That Must Not Stop":** The adventure never leaves the old ship. The smooth sea is a lie: fog banks roll in carrying boarders — drowned fleets, a rival ferry offering a "faster way home", passengers nobody remembers embarking. Something vast follows in the ship's wake, patient, closer each night: the part of death that does not accept the Leviathan's bargain. The party must keep the failing ship sailing; the tension is between trusting the silent old man and the boarders' whispered offers. The finale is a storm crossing where the pursuer catches up. Claustrophobic, escalating siege horror.

## Stakes & Clock

**The Night Ladder.** There is no sun — but periodically the grey twilight dims to true black: a "night". Each night the pursuer manifests one step closer, and the ship visibly decays in step. Party actions move them up or down the ladder ± a night: stalling, stopping, or accepting boarders' bargains advance it; repairs, good watches, and hard choices slow it.

- **Night 1:** the wake behind the ship stops fading — it stretches taut to the horizon like a towline.
- **Night 2:** a second silhouette on the horizon, hull-down, matching course. The dead wounds ache.
- **Night 3:** the fog banks begin — first boarders. The old man starts sleeping (he never slept before).
- **Night 4:** the pursuer's bow wave is visible; the sea slopes gently backwards toward it, as if downhill.
- **Night 5:** it is close enough to *hear* — the groan of a breaking hull (the Warden's sound from Adventure I). Storm rising.
- **Night 6:** the storm crossing — the finale; it catches up.

**Permanent loss:** a soul the pursuer takes — a PC who accepts a boarder's bargain, falls overboard, or is caught in the finale — becomes one of the **drowned sailors**, the same things that swarmed the beach in the prologue. Final death; the party may later face what was taken, wearing a friend's face.

**Who feels it besides the party:**
- **The nameless passengers** — other souls aboard, whom nobody remembers embarking. If the ship falls, they are taken too; any bargain the party strikes may be paid in passengers.
- **The old man** — every night of the chase drains him; he visibly ages, and by the finale the human shape is failing. He is spending his newborn strength on this debt.
- **The living world** — time passes strangely ashore. While they sail, Rauthorn survives, tells his own story of the island, and moves toward the ritual. The stakes include *what they return to* (sets up Adventure III).

## Antagonist

**The Undertow — death's law given driftwood shape.** At first a smudge on the horizon; by Night 4, a shape: not one ship but hundreds — galleons grown into frigates grown into longboats, keels jutting like broken ribs, a mountain of everything the sea ever swallowed, sailing *without sails*. Lanterns burn green in a thousand portholes: its collected souls. It is slow. It never turns. It never stops. The drowned sailors of the prologue were its fingertips.

**Wants:** the party's souls, **because** they drowned and drowned souls are owed to it — the ferryman is a thief and the debt must balance. The motive stands with or without this party; it has collected every wreck since the sea began.

**Voice:** it speaks rarely — through boarders, or through the water in the bilge — and it is polite, patient, and correct: *"Nothing is being done to you. Something was done to IT. You drowned. You are owed to me. The ferryman is a thief."* The horror: it isn't wrong.

**The boarders are its fingers.** Every fog-bank boarder — drowned crews, the rival ferry with its "faster way home", sweet-voiced offers in the dark — is the Undertow reaching forward. All their bargains are its bargains; anyone who accepts is collected. The "faster way home" is the trap that punishes disbelieving the old man.

**Bargaining — a soul for a soul, with a hidden price.** Death's law is arithmetic, not malice: it is owed N souls and will accept *any* N. The nameless passengers can be given in the party's place — and the Undertow keeps every bargain to the letter. **The twist (GM only):** every passenger is an embodied gift of the Leviathan — a new ability, an attribute point, a rare magic item it means to grant each soul it lands alive. A sacrificed passenger removes that gift from the game forever. The players are *teased* that sacrifice has consequences — the old man grows quieter with each one given, says only things like *"That was not yours to spend"* — but the rule is never explained until the landing.

**Passenger design:** each passenger embodies one *kind* of gift — and that gift goes to **every PC**, so sacrificing a single passenger costs the whole party that entire gift category. Their personas obliquely mirror individual party members to build attachment (a fencing master who drills the fighter each morning; a quiet girl humming a song only the bard's character would know; a cartographer sketching coastlines that don't exist yet; a gambler who wins every hand and folds at the end). Each remembers boarding differently — and wrongly. The roster is drafted against the real party at build time.

**The signature objects (plant now, pay off in Adventure III):** every passenger is never seen without one object — the same object the party will later find stowed on the gift boat as that passenger's gift (see Rewards). The Fencing Master takes one small pull from an exotic, unlabeled bottle after every morning drill and offers it to no one; the Gambler rolls the same strange coins across his knuckles hand after hand; the Sleeping Man sleeps curled around a sea chest nobody has ever seen opened; the Cartographer keeps her sketches rolled in a bone tube she taps when she thinks; the Quiet Girl is always working at her object (form fixed at build). The objects are pure set dressing in Adventure II — never called out mechanically — so that opening the boat's stores in Adventure III lands as recognition, not exposition: *this is the Fencing Master's bottle.* A sacrificed passenger's object vanishes with them, and its absence from the boat is how the party learns, days later, exactly what they spent.

## Locations

**Prologue:**
- **The collapsing Temple of the Deep** — the escape, rooms shaking apart in reverse order; the vision pool now a glowing passage. Smell of deep water rushing *up*.
- **The beach** — the unwinnable fight; drowned sailors in the surf, Rauthorn's blue-lit eyes in the firelight.

**The ketch (name at build) — the adventure's only stage and poster image.** Above decks: a weathered two-masted fishing boat, absurdly small for the crossing; she creaks wrong — always the same three creaks, in the same order, like a heartbeat. Below decks she doesn't end: more doors than the hull has length. Nobody aboard comments on it. **As she decays night by night, the inside *shrinks*** — doors gone, stairs shorter — as if the ship were forgetting itself.

- **The helm & the lantern** — the old man's post; a lantern that burns without oil, the only warm color in the world. Boarders will not step into its circle. It dims as he weakens.
- **The passenger hold** — an always-lit stove that is never fed; the passengers' looping routines; the smell of tea nobody brews. Where the party learns to care about the gifts they may have to spend.
- **The lowest door / the bilge** — below the waterline, a door the old man asks them to keep shut. Behind it: standing dark water where the Undertow speaks and reflections misbehave worst. Temptation and truth in one room.
- **The crow's nest** — from the masthead, and only from there, sharp eyes glimpse the *other* sea: sails of the living world, gulls, a coastline. Proof the destination is real; agony that it's so far.

**The landing — the beach where they died.** Through the storm-wall lies the prologue's beach at true dawn — the adventure's first sunlight. They wade ashore past the battle's debris and wake gasping among the dead where their bodies fell. Days have passed ashore; the Tideblade is gone — and the party is stranded on the island. **The ferryman's last gift:** anchored in the shallows rides a small, weathered fishing boat, seaworthy and empty — no crew, no name on her stern, provisions and water stowed for exactly the party's number. And she is *familiar*: the same lines as the ketch, the same patched tan sail, small enough to be real — as if the ship that carried them had a little sister, or had shrunk down to something the living world could hold. Everything about her is deniable (a boat lost from the Tideblade in the storm? a fisherman's craft driven here and abandoned?), but her ropes are dry when everything else on the beach is soaked. She is the party's way off the island — and one more thread to pull.

## Structure

Four acts mapped to the night ladder (3–4 sessions):

- **Act 1 — The Drowning (prologue, ~half session):** escape the collapsing temple (the dungeon's rooms in reverse, now hazards), dive the vision-pool passage, surface at the beach mid-battle, Rauthorn's accusation, the unwinnable fight, death. **Staging the death — last-stand vignettes:** real rounds, real rolls, real damage, but Rauthorn has no stat block (like the Warden) and the crew is endless; when each PC drops, they get a short spotlight — one final act that *matters* (shielding a friend, spitting defiance, a last word Rauthorn will remember in Adventure III). Death is certain; how they die is theirs. **The rebirth vision:** between death and waking — darkness, cold, the sensation of sinking; then the vision-pool image from Adventure I made real: a storm-torn black sea and a gigantic figure rising out of it, water falling from its flanks like collapsing cliffs, eyes burning like two cold stars. It is being *born* — the sea recoils from it. Its gaze finds each of them, and something passes through them that is not words but feels like a debt being acknowledged. Then a vast eye slides past in the dark, close enough to touch. This is the only dream of the entire adventure (aboard, they never dream — the contrast is itself a hint), and it stays deniable as a drowning dream. *Ends: waking on the ketch to the same three creaks.*
- **Act 2 — The Smooth Sea (Nights 0–2):** the cover story; meeting the passengers and learning their loops; ship routines and watches; the cracks appear (wounds, sky, reflections, hunger); the wake goes taut, a silhouette rises. *Ends: the first fog bank rolls in — and the Pilot's boat is in it.*
- **Act 3 — The Fog Banks (Nights 3–5):** boarder waves — the Pilot's offer, the dead Tideblade crewmates, drowned things; the ship shrinks and must be kept sailing; the bilge door tempts; the old man weakens; first chance to bargain passengers away. **Midpoint reversal — the crow's nest sighting:** from the masthead they glimpse the living sea, and on it the *Tideblade* under full sail, Rauthorn at the helm, untouched. If they were rescued survivors, why is their ship over *there* while they sail *this*? The rescue story dies; "we are dead" becomes the best remaining theory — still never confirmed. *Ends: Night 5 — the breaking-hull groan, storm rising.*
- **Act 4 — The Storm Crossing (Night 6):** the Undertow draws level inside the storm and states the debt, correct to the letter: souls were taken from it, and it will have their number — *any* souls aboard count. While boarders swarm the deck, the party chooses what crosses and what is given. *Ends: the wall of black water, dawn, waking among the dead on the beach — the Leviathan's gifts wait as cargo aboard the gift boat, to be discovered in Adventure III.* **The last glimpse (epilogue):** as they wake gasping, the ketch is simply *gone* — no wreck, no sail, no wake. Far out, at the edge of the dawn haze, something vast breaches once — a dark back, sunlit spray — and dives. It could have been a whale. The GM never says otherwise. Then they find the fishing boat waiting in the shallows — the ferryman's last gift (see The landing, above): their way off the island, wearing the ketch's own lines. Players who assembled the ferryman clue trail (below) will know what they saw and what they've been given; the others carry the question home. Neither the old man's identity nor their death is ever confirmed.

**The ferryman mystery (clue trail, seeded across the voyage).** The old man's identity is a puzzle the players unravel, never a reveal. The build seeds these and lets the table connect them:
- **He has no name.** Asked, he answers differently each time — and every name he gives is an old word for the sea. The passengers each remember a different name too, all wrong.
- **The lantern's light** pulses with the same slow bluish beat as the temple's veined walls and the Shard's glow — players have seen that pulse twice before.
- **When he touches the water** — washing his hands, trailing his fingers — the sea goes glass-smooth to the horizon. Nobody remarks on it.
- **His shadow on the sail**, in lantern-lit glimpses, is too large and wrong-shaped. Looking directly, it's just an old man's shadow.
- **The drowned fear this ship.** Boarders will not name him; the Undertow calls him only "the ferryman" and "the thief"; the dead crewmates whisper that the drowned give this hull a wide berth.
- **In the storm crossing**, one lightning-lit instant: his eyes flash like two cold stars — the signature of the figure from the visions. One glimpse, storm-lit, deniable.
- **The tokens** — the small wrong objects each PC carries back bear, when someone finally looks closely, a tiny scratched wave-mark: the "born in the depths of the sea" symbol from the Gate of Three Bindings.
- **The boat in the shallows** — the fishing boat waiting at the landing has the ketch's lines, her patched tan sail, and — for anyone who sails her long enough to notice — the same three creaks, in the same order, like a heartbeat they already know. Low on her transom, half-hidden under old paint: the scratched wave-mark. She is also the only way off the island, so the players *will* spend time aboard their biggest clue.

**The Toll (the finale's forced choice).** The options on the table:
- **Give passengers** — each one given is a gift deleted from the game (its cargo is simply missing from the gift boat, revealed in Adventure III).
- **Give one of themselves** — drowned forever; the party may meet them again wearing the Undertow's green light.
- **Accept the old man's quiet offer** — he can settle part of the debt with a piece of his newborn strength. *Not* his existence — the Leviathan survives, its role in the campaign (and whether it is good or evil) stays open — but the campaign gets **measurably harder**: a weakened Leviathan means less protection and aid against Rauthorn's ritual in the adventures ahead. The players must be shown this has teeth.
- **Deciding slowly is also a choice** — each round of delay, the storm takes something.

## Rewards

**The passengers ARE the treasure.** Each passenger embodies one gift the Leviathan grants — **to every PC**. Gifts survive only if their passenger does; every passenger bargained away to the Undertow deletes that entire gift category for the whole party.

**How the gifts are delivered — cargo aboard the gift boat.** The gifts are not handed over in a scene; they are *stowed on the fishing boat* waiting in the shallows, found as the party sails her — and discovering and redeeming them is **Adventure III content** (flag for that build). Crucially, each item is the **signature object** its passenger carried all voyage (see Passenger design in Cast) — the party has watched these things in living hands for six nights, so finding them in the stores needs no explanation at all. Each surviving passenger's gift takes a mundane, deniable physical form aboard: the Sleeping Man's magic items lie wrapped in oilcloth in a sea chest under the aft bench; the Fencing Master's attribute point is an exotic, unlabeled bottle of liquor — one deep pull each, and something in them is permanently stronger; the Quiet Girl's abilities, the Gambler's coins, the Cartographer's map — all packed as ordinary ship's stores that turn out to be anything but. A sacrificed passenger's cargo simply isn't aboard; nothing marks the empty space where it would have been. Like everything else, the cargo is deniable — well-provisioned boats carry chests and bottles — but the count is always exactly right for the party.

The gift menu (roster keyed to the real party at build time; physical form = what's found stowed aboard the gift boat):
- **The Fencing Master** → a permanent attribute point / Shadowdark talent for each PC — *aboard: an exotic, unlabeled bottle of liquor; one deep pull each and the bonus is permanent.*
- **The Sleeping Man** → a rare sea-flavored magic item of real weight for each PC — nobody knows what he is until the end; the biggest gift sleeps deepest — *aboard: a sea chest under the aft bench, items wrapped in oilcloth.*
- **The Quiet Girl** → a new ability for each PC (a spell, a knack, a song of the deep — flavored per character at build) — *aboard: form decided at Adventure III build (e.g. a bundle of knotted cords, a song scratched inside the hull).*
- **The Gambler** → "Luck of the Deep": a re-roll token per PC (the gambler's coin), potentially recharging at sea in later adventures — *aboard: a small purse of strange coins, one per PC.*
- **The Cartographer** → knowledge: a map of coastlines that don't exist yet, marking where Rauthorn's ritual will happen — information as treasure and Adventure III's hook — *aboard: rolled in a bone tube lashed under the gunwale.*

**What crosses back:** each PC wakes clutching one small object from the ship — a coin, a mug still warm, a scrap of sail — mundane and unremarkable, except it's dry when everything else is soaked, or salt-crusted when nothing else is. Never explained. Plus each PC's death-wound as a pale scar that aches near deep water. The "was it real?" question stays alive forever.

## Tone & Table

**Eerie, gloomy, ambiguous.** The players should be able to guess they are dead but never receive confirmation — **ambiguity is a hard constraint on every scene**: no NPC, read-aloud, or mechanic may state it outright. Grim, nautical, occult continuity with Adventure I.

- **Levels:** 3–5 (enter at 3, land at ~5). **Party size:** 3–5. **Sessions:** 3–4.
- **Off-limits:** grim but not graphic — dread, drowning imagery, and hard choices, but no lingering gore or torture scenes. The Quiet Girl stays in (her possible sacrifice is *meant* to hurt), handled by implication, never on-screen cruelty.

## Open Threads

- **The Leviathan's true nature** — good, evil, or something older than both? It repaid its debt; that proves bookkeeping, not kindness. Its regathering strength — and whatever was tolled away at the storm-wall — carries into the rest of the campaign.
- **Rauthorn's new path to the ritual** — the Shard is destroyed, so the ritual book must name another way (the Leviathan itself now swims free…). What Rauthorn told the world about the island, and his next move, open Adventure III.
- **The Undertow keeps ledgers** — it never forgets souls that escaped it. Any time a PC nearly drowns in later adventures, something in the water recognizes them.
- **"Are we dead?" — never answered** — the ambiguity survives permanently: no NPC, vision, or god ever confirms what happened between the beach and the dawn. A campaign-level rule, not just this adventure's.
- **The gift boat** — the party's new vessel is a standing clue that sails with them into Adventure III (they'll need her to follow the Cartographer's map). Whether she is an ordinary boat that happens to resemble the ketch, or something the ferryman left behind, is never settled; if she is ever wrecked or given up, the sea takes her quietly — no wreckage is ever found.
- **The Leviathan's gifts are cargo, not a scene** — **do not forget at the Adventure III build:** the gifts of every surviving passenger are stowed aboard the gift boat in mundane, deniable form (see Rewards), and the party discovers and redeems them early in Adventure III. Only surviving passengers' cargo is aboard.
